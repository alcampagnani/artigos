Brazil Legal Events 2026: A Strategic Guide for US Counsel

Why US lawyers and companies should track Brazilian legal events

American practitioners often treat Brazilian legal developments as something to react to. A Supreme Court decision lands. A new statute passes. A regulatory agency issues a normative instruction. By the time the news reaches a US client through translation, the strategic window has closed.

Reading upstream signals works better. Brazil legal events 2026 generate exactly that kind of signal.

Resolutions become rules

The OAB Federal Council and its state chapters do not formally make law. They influence it powerfully anyway. Resolutions adopted at major bar gatherings often appear, almost verbatim, in legislative proposals or judicial guidelines that follow.

The 2022 Brazilian Bar conference produced a series of motions on platform liability. Within fourteen months, three of those motions had been picked up by congressional committees drafting what later became part of the platform regulation debate still active today. American technology companies operating in Brazil that monitored the conference saw the trajectory months ahead of competitors.

Academic congresses telegraph judicial trends

The Brazilian international law congress matters for a different reason. Brazilian appellate judges, especially at the Superior Court of Justice (Superior Tribunal de Justiça, or STJ, the highest non-constitutional federal court), routinely cite scholarship presented at the Congresso Brasileiro de Direito Internacional (CBDI). The congress is organized by ABDI (Academia Brasileira de Direito Internacional, the Brazilian Academy of International Law), the Brazilian branch of the International Law Association.

When Professor Valesca Borges Moschen or her colleagues publish on a doctrinal question, that publication often surfaces in STJ decisions on cross-border contract disputes, recognition of foreign judgments, and applicable law to international commercial agreements. For US firms drafting Brazilian arbitration clauses or recognition petitions, that is operational intelligence.

The compliance lag is real

Most US compliance teams find out about Brazilian regulatory shifts ninety to one hundred eighty days after they happen. By that point, exposure has accumulated. Tracking Brazil legal events 2026 compresses that lag from months to weeks. Of the available Brazil legal events 2026, the two profiled below produce the densest signal-to-noise ratio.

24th Brazilian Congress of International Law: Vitória, August 2026

The 24th Brazilian Congress of International Law (24º Congresso Brasileiro de Direito Internacional, or 24º CBDI) runs from August 26 through August 29, 2026, at UFES (Universidade Federal do Espírito Santo, the Federal University of Espírito Santo) in Vitória, the capital of Espírito Santo state. Professor Dr. Valesca Borges Moschen, a leading voice in Brazilian private international law, serves as executive coordinator.

Why Vitória matters this year

Espírito Santo sits between Rio de Janeiro and Bahia on the southeastern coast. The state hosts the Tubarão port complex, operated by mining giant Vale, and absorbs substantial foreign direct investment in mining, steel, and oil and gas. Choosing UFES as the 2026 venue is not accidental. The host university has built one of Brazil’s stronger international law faculties, and the regional economy gives the academic discussions a practical edge that congresses held in São Paulo or Rio sometimes lack.

For US lawyers handling foreign direct investment, port logistics disputes, or commodities arbitration, the geography alone is worth the trip.

Program structure and what to expect

The CBDI traditionally splits between public and private international law tracks. Public sessions cover treaty interpretation, sovereign immunity, international organizations, and human rights. Private sessions handle conflict of laws, international commercial arbitration, recognition of foreign judgments, cross-border family law, and digital trade.

The call for papers closed on February 20, 2026. By that deadline, the academic committee receives several hundred submissions from Brazilian and international scholars. Selection results are scheduled for publication on July 15, 2026. Selected papers feed both the panel sessions and the official congress publication.

The book launch worth noting

The congress will launch Direito Internacional em Expansão (International Law in Expansion), a multi-volume series that has become a standard reference for Brazilian international law scholarship. The 2026 volumes will include contributions on AI and international law, cross-border data flows under the LGPD (Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados, Brazil’s General Data Protection Law, formally Law No. 13.709/2018) and analogous foreign frameworks, and open questions in international commercial arbitration involving Brazilian parties.

US lawyers should care because Brazilian courts cite this series often. If you litigate or arbitrate against Brazilian counterparties, you should own the relevant volumes.

Strategic value for US practitioners

Three concrete takeaways from past CBDI editions illustrate why Brazil legal events 2026 like this one earn calendar space.

The 22nd CBDI in 2022 hosted extensive debate on the Hague Judgments Convention. Brazil has not ratified it, but the academic positioning at that congress predicted the cautious posture later adopted by the Brazilian foreign ministry. US firms advising clients on enforcement strategy adjusted accordingly.

The 23rd CBDI featured panels on cryptocurrency and conflict of laws. Within a year, Brazilian first-instance courts began citing the academic frameworks discussed there in early disputes involving cross-border crypto transactions.

Family law sessions at recent CBDI editions have changed how Brazilian courts handle international child abduction cases under the Hague Convention, including matters involving US habitual residence claims. American family lawyers handling cases with a Brazilian dimension benefit from the doctrinal positions surfaced at these sessions.

25th National Conference of Brazilian Advocacy: Salvador, November 2026

The 25th National Conference of Brazilian Advocacy (25ª Conferência Nacional da Advocacia Brasileira) returns to Salvador, Bahia, from November 23 through November 25, 2026, at the Salvador Convention Center (Centro de Convenções de Salvador, located at Av. Octávio Mangabeira, 5490 - Boca do Rio). The event is the largest gathering in the Brazil legal calendar and arguably the largest Brazilian advocacy conference in the world.

A Guinness World Record for scale

The 2024 edition drew 22,000 participants, securing a Guinness World Record for the largest legal event ever held. The 2026 conference is on track to match or exceed that figure. Programming spans 50 panels, 35 special events, and 400 confirmed speakers across three days, plus roughly 180 exhibition booths.

For context, the American Bar Association annual meeting typically draws between 8,000 and 12,000 registered attendees. The Brazilian conference is roughly twice that size.

The 2026 theme

This year’s theme reads, in Portuguese, Direito e Tecnologia: O Futuro da Advocacia na Sociedade Digital (Law and Technology: The Future of Legal Practice in the Digital Society). Every track will route, in some way, through technology, AI, platform regulation, data protection, or digital evidence.

For US counsel, that theme is the most important signal of the year. Whatever positions emerge from Salvador will pressure Brazilian lawmakers, regulators, and courts on issues that directly affect US technology companies, financial services firms, and any business processing personal data of Brazilians.

Leadership and the Salvador return

Beto Simonetti, president of the OAB Federal Council, leads the 2026 conference together with Daniela Borges, president of the OAB Bahia chapter. The choice to bring the conference back to Salvador after a 24-year gap is symbolically important. Bahia historically anchors the Northeast’s legal and political weight in Brazil, and a Salvador edition draws northeastern lawyers in greater numbers than southern editions.

The Rui Barbosa Medal

The conference will award the Rui Barbosa Medal, the highest honor in Brazilian advocacy conference tradition, to Marcus Vinicius Furtado Coêlho (a former OAB Federal Council president) and Vera Lúcia Santana Araújo (a longtime racial-justice and human rights advocate). Both recipients have long-standing reputations in constitutional law and human rights advocacy. Their recognition signals the political register the OAB will adopt during the event.

Cross-cutting themes affecting US practice in Brazil

Both Brazil legal events 2026 will surface a set of overlapping themes that touch US practice. The smart play is mapping these themes against your own client portfolio before the events begin.

Artificial intelligence regulation

Brazil’s pending AI legislation has been working through the National Congress for several sessions. The Senate’s Bill PL 2338/2023, the country’s main legislative vehicle for an artificial intelligence framework, remains the most prominent proposal. Both the Vitória congress and the Salvador conference will host extensive debate on AI governance, liability for autonomous systems, and the relationship between AI regulation and professional ethics.

US AI companies operating in Brazil, or planning to, should expect the OAB to adopt formal positions in Salvador. Those positions will pressure Congress and the executive branch.

Data protection and LGPD enforcement

The ANPD (Autoridade Nacional de Proteção de Dados, the Brazilian National Data Protection Authority) has steadily increased enforcement activity. Recent administrative proceedings against major platforms hint at the trajectory.

Conference panels will address international data transfers, the adequacy framework, processor liability, and the intersection between LGPD and emerging AI rules. The discussion sits alongside the Marco Civil da Internet (Law No. 12.965/2014, Brazil’s Internet Civil Framework), which still anchors platform liability and intermediary obligations. US companies subject to LGPD should brief their legal teams on the conclusions reached in Salvador within thirty days of the conference.

International cooperation in civil and criminal matters

The CBDI traditionally devotes substantial programming to mutual legal assistance, international rogatory letters, and the recognition of foreign judgments. The 24th edition will likely revisit the 2024 reforms to the Código de Processo Civil (Civil Procedure Code) provisions on international cooperation.

For US litigators seeking discovery from Brazil or enforcement of US judgments, the doctrinal positions at Vitória will inform STJ practice for years.

Foreign investment and arbitration

Brazil’s investment treaty position, its arbitration framework, and the role of the Comitê Nacional de Investimentos (National Investments Committee) will surface across both events. The CBDI will treat these issues academically. The Salvador conference will treat them politically.

US private equity sponsors, joint venture partners, and infrastructure investors should track the arbitration sessions especially carefully. Recent STJ decisions on the recognition of foreign arbitral awards have introduced friction that did not exist five years ago.

Platform liability and the gig economy

Brazil’s debate on platform worker classification continues. The TST (Tribunal Superior do Trabalho, the Superior Labor Court) has issued mixed signals. Both events will host panels on this question.

US companies operating gig platforms in Brazil should expect strong positions from the OAB advocating for some form of formal recognition of platform-mediated work relationships. The political weight of the conference will likely accelerate legislative activity.

How to use these events to strengthen US-Brazil legal strategy

Knowing about Brazil legal events 2026 is one thing. Extracting strategic value is another. Serious US practices tend to follow a similar playbook.

Before the events: portfolio mapping

Sixty days out, audit your Brazilian exposure. Which clients have Brazilian subsidiaries? Which have Brazilian counterparties? Which face pending Brazilian litigation or regulatory matters?

For each, identify the two or three issues most likely to surface at Vitória or Salvador. AI policy. LGPD enforcement. Arbitration recognition. Cross-border family matters. Tax cooperation. Then assign someone, internally or externally, to monitor those specific tracks.

During the events: real-time intelligence

Both events generate real-time output. The OAB publishes daily summaries of the Salvador conference. The CBDI releases panel materials and abstracts during the event. Social media coverage, especially LinkedIn, captures granular detail on individual speakers and motions.

A US firm without Portuguese-language capacity will struggle. We hear it regularly from in-house counsel: “We saw the headline three weeks later in English.” That delay is expensive.

After the events: resolution analysis and action

Within thirty days of each event, the OAB publishes formal resolutions and the CBDI releases conference proceedings. Read them. Map them against your client portfolio. Issue client alerts where warranted.

Within ninety days, the legislative and judicial spillover begins. Congressional committees pick up resolutions. Judicial training programs incorporate doctrinal positions. Regulatory agencies float consultations on policy directions debated at the events.

The window for US firms to either build advantage or fall behind opens here.
