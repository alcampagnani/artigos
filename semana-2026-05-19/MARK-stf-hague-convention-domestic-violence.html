Hague Convention Brazil: STF Carves Out Abuse Exception

What the STF Actually Decided in ADIs 4245 and 7686

On August 27, 2025, the Brazilian Supreme Court ruled jointly on two ações diretas de inconstitucionalidade (ADIs, direct actions of unconstitutionality): ADI 4245, filed years earlier by the now-extinct Partido Democratas, and ADI 7686, filed by Partido Socialismo e Liberdade (PSOL). Both challenged how Brazilian courts apply the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction. Justice Luís Roberto Barroso served as rapporteur.

The headline result surprised nobody who had been watching the case. The Hague Convention Brazil framework remains in force. The Court confirmed the treaty enjoys status supralegal (supralegal status), meaning it sits above ordinary federal statutes and is fully compatible with the 1988 Federal Constitution. Brazil keeps its treaty obligations to the other 100-plus member states.

What changes is the application. The Court issued a binding interpretation of Article 13(b), the Convention’s “grave risk” exception. Before this ruling, Brazilian federal judges treated Article 13(b) inconsistently. Some demanded near-conclusive proof of imminent physical harm to the child. Others looked at family dynamics broadly. The STF resolved that split.

The New Standard: Plausibility, Not Proof Beyond Doubt

Justice Barroso’s lead opinion sets a clearer evidentiary bar. Brazilian courts must deny return when “objective and concrete indications of domestic violence” exist, even if the child is not a direct victim. Witnessing violence against the mother counts. Living in a household marked by coercive control counts.

The standard is plausibility of grave risk, not irrefutable proof. Victim testimony combined with objective evidence is sufficient. That objective evidence can include police reports, medical records, protective orders, witness statements, and school counselor notes. The Court explicitly rejected the older approach that required a smoking gun.

Public and notorious facts about gender-based violence in the country of origin also qualify as evidence. If the requesting state has documented patterns of failing to protect victims, judges in Brazil may consider that.

Key Data: Research cited by the STF found that 88 percent of women involved in international child abduction cases are themselves victims of domestic violence by the left-behind parent. The Court used this number to justify its gender-perspective interpretation of Article 13(b).

Mandatory Gender-Perspective Interpretation

The STF mandated that all Hague Convention Brazil cases be analyzed through perspectiva de gênero (gender perspective), a methodology already used by Brazilian criminal courts in Lei Maria da Penha (the federal domestic violence statute) cases.

In practice, the gender-perspective lens means judges must account for the domestic environment where abuse happens behind closed doors, recognize victim isolation including cultural and linguistic barriers, and understand that the inability to produce traditional evidence does not equal absence of abuse.

The Advocacia-Geral da União (AGU, the federal government’s legal arm) defended this position. AGU lawyer Rodrigo Carmona put it bluntly during oral argument: “An aggressor will never be a good father. An aggressor exposes the mother and child to serious risk.” Justice Barroso quoted that line back during deliberations.

Procedural Reforms Attached to the Ruling

The decision is not just doctrine. It comes with operational mandates. The Conselho Nacional de Justiça (CNJ, the national judicial council) has 60 days to set up an interinstitutional task force coordinating courts, the AGU, the Federal Police, and child protection authorities.

Federal Regional Courts (the Tribunais Regionais Federais, TRFs) must concentrate Hague jurisdiction in specialized units. Today, any Vara Federal (federal trial court) can hear a Hague case. After the ruling, only designated specialized chambers will handle them. Practitioners had asked for this change for years. It finally has constitutional force behind it.

The Court also imposed a one-year cap from filing to final decision. The Convention itself sets a six-week guideline. Brazil has historically blown past that mark. The new ceiling is a hard outer limit, not a target. In practice, this single procedural change may do more for cases on the ground than the doctrinal shift. The cap forces trial judges into earlier evidentiary cuts and tighter scheduling orders whether they like it or not.

How the Hague Convention Brazil Framework Has Worked Until Now

To understand why the STF intervened, you need the back story. Brazil acceded to the Hague Convention in 1999 and promulgated it domestically through Decreto 3.413/2000. The Autoridade Central Administrativa Federal (ACAF, the Federal Central Administrative Authority), housed in the Ministério da Justiça e Segurança Pública (Ministry of Justice and Public Security), processes applications.

When an American parent files a Hague petition, the U.S. State Department transmits it to ACAF, which routes it to the AGU. The AGU then files suit in Justiça Federal (Federal Court) in the district where the child is located. The case is federal because it involves a treaty obligation. The Hague proceeding is narrower than an ordinary custody case: it asks only whether the child should return to the country of habitual residence.

The Persistent Delay Problem

The Convention’s six-week target became a running joke among practitioners. Brazilian federal cases routinely took two to four years. The reasons varied: backlogged dockets, generalist judges unfamiliar with the treaty, aggressive use of agravos (interlocutory appeals), and slow psychological evaluations.

For left-behind American parents, the delay alone often meant losing the case on the merits. Children who have lived in Brazil for two years form attachments and learn Portuguese. By the time the case is ready for decision, the Article 12 “settled in” exception starts to look attractive to a sympathetic judge.

Article 13(b) Before the 2025 Ruling

Article 13(b) of the Hague Convention permits a court to refuse return when there is “a grave risk that his or her return would expose the child to physical or psychological harm or otherwise place the child in an intolerable situation.” The text is the same in every signatory state. The application is not.

In Brazil, before August 2025, the case law went both ways. Some federal judges and TRF panels read Article 13(b) narrowly, requiring proof that returning the child specifically would cause concrete, identifiable harm. Others took a broader view, treating exposure to a violent home environment as sufficient. No binding STF precedent resolved the split.

The result was a geographic lottery. A Hague case heard in São Paulo might come out differently from the same case in Belo Horizonte or Recife. Experienced practitioners learned to factor venue into strategy. The 2025 ruling reduces that guesswork.

For a U.S. attorney handling international family matters, the table above is the executive summary. The shift favors the parent resisting return when domestic violence is part of the record.

The New Domestic Violence Exception in Practice

The doctrine is one thing. How federal judges actually apply it in the cases coming through over the next twelve to eighteen months is what matters for clients. Here is what U.S. counsel should expect.

Who Qualifies as a Protected Parent

The ruling does not create a free pass for any parent claiming abuse. The standard is “objective and concrete indications.” Judges will look for documentary anchors: police reports from the country of origin, medical records reflecting injuries, protective orders (even temporary ones), text messages, and witness statements.

The taking parent does not need a criminal conviction or a final protective order. What she needs is enough corroboration to lift her testimony above bare assertion.

A parent walking into a Brazilian federal court with only her own testimony will struggle. A parent with police reports from Houston, two pediatrician notes, and a temporary restraining order will not.

Practical Note: U.S. courts and Brazilian courts handle evidentiary records differently. A protective order from Texas needs apostila (apostille) certification under the 1961 Hague Apostille Convention to be admissible without further authentication. American attorneys preparing materials for Brazilian Hague cases should build apostille time into the schedule. Plan on two to four weeks per document.

How Judges Will Likely Balance Risk Against Return

Even with the new exception, the Convention’s core principle survives. Brazilian courts must still favor return absent grave risk. The shift is in how they weigh evidence, not in whether they apply Article 13(b).

In close cases, expect judges to order targeted measures: supervised visitation in the country of origin, court-monitored handover, or conditional return tied to specific protective measures by the requesting state. Brazilian federal courts have used these tools sparingly in the past. The 2025 ruling encourages them.

For American practitioners, the practical takeaway is that pure return-or-no-return outcomes will become rarer in any Hague Convention Brazil case with a domestic violence component. Negotiated, conditional outcomes will become more common.

What This Means for American Parents

The decision affects two very different groups of Americans. Left-behind parents whose children were taken to Brazil now face a tougher path to a return order. Fleeing parents who came to Brazil to escape abuse now have a clearer legal lane to keep their children safe. Both groups need to recalibrate.

Left-Behind American Parents

If your child has been wrongfully removed from the United States to Brazil, the Hague Convention Brazil framework still works. It just works differently.

Anticipate domestic violence allegations. Even if your relationship was conflict-ridden but not violent, expect the taking parent to frame it as such. Preserve emails, texts, and recordings that show the actual tone of the relationship. Anything written by the taking parent acknowledging your role as a parent is gold.

Be ready to address country-of-origin conditions. Brazilian judges will look at U.S. domestic violence enforcement and take judicial notice of high-profile cases. Your counsel must distinguish your home jurisdiction from generalized critiques of the United States.

And do not delay. The one-year cap means the case moves faster than before. The window to organize evidence, engage experts, and brief Brazilian counsel is narrower than the old practice allowed.

American Parents Who Fled to Brazil

For mothers (and occasionally fathers) who left the United States with children to escape abuse, the ruling provides legal architecture that previously existed only in theory.

The exception is not automatic. Filing a Hague defense requires building the same kind of record you would in a U.S. domestic violence case. Documentation collected before you left is significantly more persuasive than what is assembled after. Police reports from the U.S. carry weight; affidavits drafted in Brazil three months later carry less.

You also need to be careful about what you say in U.S. proceedings. Statements made in U.S. divorce or custody actions are admissible in Brazilian Hague cases. The two records cannot diverge. American parents in this position need counsel who can coordinate strategy across both jurisdictions.

Warning: Bringing a child to Brazil to escape abuse without first consulting cross-border family law counsel often complicates the legal posture. U.S. courts may issue contempt orders, ne exeat (Latin: “let him not depart”) injunctions barring removal of the child from the jurisdiction, or even arrest warrants. These instruments do not vanish in Brazil. They affect immigration status, future travel, and the Hague case itself. The right move is to plan the move with counsel, not after.

A Word on Father-Initiated Cases

The 2025 ruling uses gender-perspective language because the data drives it. The legal standard, however, is sex-neutral on its face. American fathers who took children to Brazil to escape abusive mothers can invoke Article 13(b) on the same terms. The evidence demands are identical. Courts may scrutinize father-as-victim cases more closely, which makes documentation matter even more.

How to Prepare a Hague Case in Brazil Right Now

The procedure is not complicated, but it is unforgiving on timing. Whether you are pursuing return or defending against it, the steps below cover the work that actually moves a case.

Step One: Engage Counsel in Both Countries

Hague Convention Brazil cases live in two legal systems at once. The U.S. attorney handles the State Department application, U.S. court orders, and any related custody matter. Brazilian counsel handles the federal court proceeding, AGU coordination, and local evidentiary requirements. The two need to communicate weekly.

Choose Brazilian counsel with actual Hague experience. The federal court docket is specialized, and generalist family law attorneys rarely handle Hague matters.

Step Two: Build the Evidentiary Record Early

Before any filing, collect everything: apostilled birth and marriage certificates, school records, pediatrician records, lease or deed, utility bills, employment records, and tax returns. The Hague case turns on habitual residence and consent. Documents from the year before removal carry the most weight. If domestic violence is in play, collect every police report, medical visit record, protective order, and counselor note.

Step Three: File the Application

Left-behind parents file with the U.S. State Department’s Office of Children’s Issues. The State Department routes the application to ACAF in Brasília. ACAF reviews and forwards it to AGU. For taking parents preempting the case, no affirmative filing is required. The defense begins when AGU files suit in Federal Court.

Step Four: Federal Court Proceeding

AGU files the petition in Justiça Federal in the district where the child resides. The defendant has 15 days to respond. The court typically schedules an audiência de conciliação (conciliation hearing) within 30 to 60 days. The merits phase includes psychological evaluation of the child, expert reports, and witness testimony. Under the new one-year cap, expect this phase to run six to nine months.

Practical Checklist: Documents to gather before filing or defending a Hague Convention Brazil case: - Apostilled birth certificate of every child - Apostilled marriage certificate (if applicable) - Habitual residence proof from country of origin (12 months minimum) - Custody orders or agreements with apostille - Travel records (passports, boarding passes, immigration stamps) - Communications between parents about the move - Police and medical records if domestic violence is alleged - Sworn translations of all documents into Portuguese

Step Five: Decision and Appeal

The federal judge issues a decision after the evidentiary phase closes. Either side can appeal to the relevant Tribunal Regional Federal. A second appeal to higher courts (the Superior Tribunal de Justiça and the STF) is possible but rare. Under the 2025 ruling, the entire timeline is capped at one year from filing.

If return is ordered, AGU coordinates the handover. If return is denied, the Hague case ends. Custody questions then move to Brazilian state family court, where Brazilian residency rules apply.

Costs and Practical Logistics

A typical Hague Convention Brazil case runs between USD 25,000 and USD 80,000 in legal fees, depending on complexity. Matters with heavy cross-jurisdictional records sit at the higher end of that range. Translation, apostille, and travel add to the budget. Most American clients do not factor in the cost of multiple trips to Brazil for hearings and child evaluations. All foreign-language documents require tradução juramentada (sworn translation) by a certified translator. A typical case generates 200 to 500 pages, adding another USD 8,000 to USD 25,000 in translation costs.

After the Ruling: What Counsel Should Be Doing Now

If you have an active case filed before August 27, 2025, your Brazilian counsel should already have filed a petição (motion) invoking the new STF interpretation. The ruling applies to pending cases, not just future ones. Appeals at the TRF level can be supplemented with arguments grounded in ADIs 4245 and 7686. For cases not yet filed, the strategic calculus has changed for both sides.

Conclusion

The August 2025 STF ruling is the most significant change to the Hague Convention Brazil framework since Brazil promulgated the treaty 25 years ago. ADIs 4245 and 7686 did not weaken the Convention. They sharpened how Brazilian federal courts apply Article 13(b) when domestic violence is part of the record. American parents on both sides of these cases need to update their strategy accordingly.

The ruling rewards preparation. Parents with documented histories, consistent records across U.S. and Brazilian filings, and counsel who understand both legal systems will fare better than those who rely on the old approach. Cases that used to turn on appellate strategy and delay tactics will now turn on evidence and timing. The Hague Convention Brazil playbook that worked five years ago no longer maps cleanly to current practice. The new playbook is evidence-first, gender-aware, and time-disciplined.

The CNJ task force will issue protocols within 60 days of the ruling. Those protocols will specify how courts should handle victim interviews, child evaluations, and country-condition evidence. The first wave of post-ruling cases will set the working precedents that govern the next five years of Hague Convention Brazil practice.
