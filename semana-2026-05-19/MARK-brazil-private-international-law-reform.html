Brazil Private International Law: 2025 Reform Guide

Why Brazil’s 1942 Private International Law Is Holding US Business Back

The current statute predates television in Brazil. Decreto-Lei 4.657/1942 was enacted under President Getúlio Vargas during the Estado Novo (the “New State,” Vargas’s authoritarian regime from 1937 to 1945), four years before the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. It governs how Brazilian courts treat foreign law, foreign judgments, and cross-border legal relationships. Justice Moura Ribeiro, a member of the drafting commission, put it bluntly during the October 15 ceremony at the Superior Court of Justice (Superior Tribunal de Justiça, or STJ, Brazil’s highest court for non-constitutional federal matters): Brazil is “40 years behind” global standards in private international law.

That gap shows up in concrete ways. The 1942 LINDB uses rigid connection rules. Personal status follows domicile. Contracts follow the place of execution. Real property follows the lex rei sitae (the law of the place where the property sits). There is no general clause for party autonomy, no flexible escape clause, and no modern protection for vulnerable parties. Compare that with the EU’s Rome I Regulation (Reg. 593/2008) and Rome II Regulation (Reg. 864/2007), the Hague Conventions, or the US Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws. Brazil has been working with tools designed for a world before container shipping, before the WTO, and before the internet existed.

The Concrete Cost for US Clients

For an American business operating in Brazil, the 1942 statute creates four recurring problems. First, Brazilian state courts routinely disregard choice-of-law clauses in international contracts. Second, foreign judgments face a homologation process at the STJ (the formal recognition procedure that lets a foreign judgment take effect in Brazil) that focuses on formalities and offers little flexibility. Third, cross-border family and succession disputes default to Brazilian law in ways that surprise US estate planners. Finally, evidence-gathering across borders relies on slow letters rogatory rather than modern cooperation mechanisms such as those familiar to US litigators under 28 U.S.C. § 1782.

Each of these issues costs time and money. A homologation proceeding for a US divorce decree can stretch beyond eighteen months. Letters rogatory for document production routinely take two to three years. When a Brazilian court refuses to honor a New York choice-of-law clause, the entire litigation strategy of a US parent company can collapse overnight.

Key Data: Practitioners handling foreign judgment homologation at the STJ routinely report timelines ranging from 14 to 22 months, with a meaningful share of petitions facing substantive objections that push the process well past the two-year mark. The current LINDB framework offers no fast track, no expedited docket, and no preferential treatment for commercial matters.

Why the Reform Took 80 Years

Reform efforts existed before. Brazilian legislators proposed bills in 1964, 1994, and 2004. None passed. Brazilian academia produced excellent scholarship. Professor Carmen Tiburcio at UERJ (the State University of Rio de Janeiro) and Professor Gustavo Ferraz de Campos Monaco at USP (the University of São Paulo, Brazil’s most prominent research university) have written extensively on the topic. Yet the political will was absent. The 2024 turning point came when the Council for Sustainable Economic and Social Development convened the working group in December of that year, giving the project institutional weight it had lacked before. The working group produced its draft in roughly ten months. That speed, combined with the political backing of the Palácio do Planalto (the Brazilian executive office, equivalent to the White House), is why this version is likely to reach Congress and pass.

The previous failed attempts share a common thread. Each tried to push reform through Congress without judicial buy-in. The 2025 effort flipped the order. By starting at the STJ, with the court’s president personally endorsing the project, the drafting commission produced a text the judiciary was already prepared to apply. US clients tracking Brazil private international law should also track STJ panel decisions in 2026 and 2027. The doctrinal direction will be visible there first.

What the New General Law of Private International Law Actually Changes

The Anteprojeto covers fifteen substantive areas. Among them: international cooperation, evidence production, labor contracts, consumer law, application of foreign norms, party autonomy, vulnerable parties protection, personal status, matrimonial property regime, support obligations, civil liability, international contracts, real rights, intellectual property, and investments and securities. That is a complete rewrite of Brazil private international law, not a patch.

The drafting commission was unusually high-powered. Justice Herman Benjamin (current STJ President), Justice Luis Felipe Salomão, Justice Moura Ribeiro, and Justice Paulo Sérgio Domingues represented the judiciary. Professor Carmen Tiburcio (UERJ) and Professor Gustavo Ferraz de Campos Monaco (USP) brought academic depth. The text draws on the Hague Conventions (notably the 1965 Service Convention, the 1970 Evidence Convention, the 1961 Apostille Convention, and the 2005 Choice of Court Agreements Convention), the EU regulations, the Mexico City Convention on the Law Applicable to International Contracts (CIDIP-V, 1994, which Brazil signed but never ratified), and the UNIDROIT Principles of International Commercial Contracts. For the first time, Brazil private international law sits in the mainstream of comparative conflict-of-laws doctrine.

The Big Five Innovations

Five changes will reshape practice. First, Article 28 of the draft expressly recognizes party autonomy for international contracts. Second, the homologation regime for foreign judgments shifts from purely formal review to a substantive analysis grounded in human dignity. Third, vulnerable parties (workers, consumers, and certain family-law claimants) receive special protective rules. Fourth, international cooperation moves beyond letters rogatory toward direct judicial cooperation, on a model that resembles the discovery mechanisms US litigators rely on under 28 U.S.C. § 1782. Fifth, the bill creates flexible escape clauses, so judges can depart from rigid connection rules when justice demands it.

US clients should treat the reform as imminent rather than speculative.

Party Autonomy in International Contracts: The Real Game Changer

Article 28 of the new bill is the provision US counsel will care about most. It expressly authorizes parties to international contracts to choose the applicable law. After eight decades of debate, Brazil private international law catches up to what every US lawyer assumes is normal: if your contract picks New York law, the court applies New York law.

Under the current LINDB, choice-of-law clauses live in a gray zone. Article 9 of the LINDB states that contractual obligations are governed by the law of the place where the contract was concluded. The Brazilian Arbitration Law (Law 9.307/1996) recognizes party autonomy for arbitration. By contrast, Brazilian state courts have historically refused to apply foreign law chosen by the parties when the contract reaches them through litigation. The result is a two-tier system. Arbitration honored choice of law. Litigation did not.

What Article 28 Actually Says

The drafting commission modeled Article 28 on the Hague Principles on Choice of Law in International Commercial Contracts (2015) and the EU’s Rome I Regulation. The provision authorizes parties to choose the law of any jurisdiction with a reasonable connection. It allows partial choice, dépeçage (splitting the contract among different governing laws), and modification after signing. It also recognizes non-state law (the Lex Mercatoria, or “law merchant”; the UNIDROIT Principles; and INCOTERMS, the standard international commercial terms) as valid governing rules for arbitral disputes.

For US in-house counsel drafting cross-border supply, joint venture, or licensing deals with Brazilian counterparts, this changes the drafting calculus. A choice-of-law clause is no longer aspirational under Brazil private international law. It is enforceable in Brazilian state courts, not just in arbitration.

Two Critical Exceptions

Party autonomy does not apply universally. The bill carves out two areas where Brazilian law protects vulnerable parties: labor contracts and consumer contracts. These exceptions track the EU’s Rome I Regulation. A worker bargaining with an employer, or a consumer clicking through terms of service, lacks the bargaining power to negotiate genuine choice of law. The more protective forum law prevents abuse.

For US clients, the practical implications are sharp. A US technology company selling SaaS to Brazilian consumers cannot enforce a Delaware choice-of-law clause against a Brazilian consumer plaintiff. The Brazilian Consumer Defense Code (Código de Defesa do Consumidor, or CDC, Law 8.078/1990) will apply regardless. A US multinational employing Brazilian workers cannot use New York law to govern Brazilian employment disputes. The Consolidação das Leis do Trabalho (CLT, the Consolidation of Labor Laws, codified as Decree-Law 5.452/1943) and constitutional protections will control.

Why This Matters for Litigation Strategy

The party-autonomy reform also affects forum selection. Historically, US clients pushed for arbitration in São Paulo or for foreign-seat arbitration precisely because Brazilian state courts could not be trusted to apply chosen law. With Article 28 in force, state-court litigation in Brazil becomes viable for certain disputes, particularly when arbitration is too expensive, too slow, or when urgent injunctive relief is needed.

The flip side is real. US plaintiffs may face Brazilian counterparts who litigate aggressively in São Paulo state court, knowing the chosen US law will be applied. It will change which forum is strategically optimal for which disputes under Brazil private international law.

Cross-Border Family, Succession, and Asset Disputes Under the New Rules

US estate planners and family law attorneys consistently underestimate Brazilian conflict-of-laws exposure. Brazil private international law in family and succession matters has been particularly hostile to US planning techniques. Clients with Brazilian assets (vacation homes in Búzios, investment properties in São Paulo, or agricultural land in Mato Grosso) learn this the hard way.

Personal Status and Matrimonial Property

The 1942 LINDB anchors personal status to domicile (Article 7). The new bill keeps this connecting factor but adds flexibility. Where the strict domicile rule would produce an unjust result, courts can apply the closer-connection escape. Matrimonial property regime under the current statute follows the law of the spouses’ first matrimonial domicile, with no possibility of choice. The new bill cracks that door open. Spouses can choose, in limited circumstances, the law applicable to their property regime.

For US estate planners structuring affairs for American spouses with Brazilian residency, this is a meaningful improvement. Premarital agreements drafted under New York or Florida law can have stronger purchase. However, the bill does not allow choice that would defeat Brazilian forced-heirship rules. Legítima (the mandatory share of the estate reserved for descendants, ascendants, and the surviving spouse) remains substantively protected when Brazilian law applies.

International Succession

The succession provisions modernize without revolutionizing. The default connecting factor remains the domicile of the decedent at death. The new bill introduces a limited form of professio iuris (the Latin term for the testator’s ability to choose the law applicable to succession in the will). The chosen law must have a reasonable connection, typically nationality or habitual residence at execution.

For US clients, this matters when an American with Brazilian assets dies. Under the current LINDB, a Brazilian court applies the law of the decedent’s domicile to the entire estate. If the decedent was domiciled in Brazil, Brazilian forced-heirship rules apply to all worldwide assets. The new framework allows the testator to designate US law for assets located in Brazil, subject to the legítima override.

Foreign Judgment Recognition

The homologation regime sees significant change. Currently, the STJ reviews foreign judgments under its Internal Regulations and Articles 960-965 of the Code of Civil Procedure. Review is largely formal and checks jurisdiction, due process, the absence of offense to Brazilian public order, and coisa julgada (final judgment status, equivalent to res judicata in US practice). The reform pushes further. Substantive review now includes human dignity considerations, which lets the STJ refuse homologation when the foreign judgment produces results incompatible with constitutional values.

This works in both directions. It creates new arguments for blocking foreign judgments US plaintiffs want to enforce in Brazil. It also gives Brazilian respondents fresh tools when defending homologation petitions. Homologation litigation under the new Brazil private international law regime will be more substantive, more contested, and more expensive, but also more flexible for parties with strong equities.

Cross-Border Asset Recovery

For US litigators chasing assets across the border, the bill’s international cooperation provisions matter most. Direct judicial cooperation replaces slow letters rogatory for many evidence-gathering tasks. The bill aligns Brazil more closely with the Hague Evidence Convention (1970) framework, complementing the Hague Service Convention (1965) and the Hague Apostille Convention (1961, ratified by Brazil through Decree 8.660/2016). US litigators familiar with 28 U.S.C. § 1782 discovery (the federal statute that lets foreign tribunals obtain evidence located in the United States) will recognize the conceptual structure.

Warning: The new framework does not eliminate Brazilian banking secrecy (sigilo bancário, the statutory protection of bank account information) or judicial-deposit complexities. US counsel still need local expertise for asset tracing in Brazil. Timeline, Risks, and How US Companies Should Position Now

The drafting commission delivered its work to President Lula on December 4, 2025. From here, the path is: Executive review at the Casa Civil (the Chief of Staff office that coordinates legislative drafting, roughly 2 to 4 months); transmittal to Congress; committee review in the Chamber of Deputies; plenary vote; Senate review; and presidential sanction. A reform of this magnitude with broad institutional support typically takes 12 to 24 months from delivery to enactment.

Three Scenarios for US Counsel

The first scenario is rapid enactment within 18 months substantially intact. Contracts signed today should be drafted to take advantage of the new Brazil private international law framework once it activates. Existing contracts may benefit from amendment riders.

The second scenario is delayed enactment with partial implementation. Congress passes some provisions, modifies others, tables the most controversial. Based on Brazilian legislative history, this is the likeliest outcome.

The third scenario is judicial anticipation without enactment. Brazilian courts, especially the STJ, often anticipate reform when the direction is doctrinally clear. STJ panels have already cited the Anteprojeto in 2025 decisions. Doctrinal change is coming regardless of the legislative timeline.

Six Action Items Before Year-End

US counsel with Brazilian exposure should treat the next 12 months as a planning window. Concrete steps:

  1. Audit cross-border contract portfolios for choice-of-law and forum-selection clauses. Identify weak language that would not survive even an Article 28 analysis.
  2. Reassess arbitration versus state-court litigation strategy in light of the coming party-autonomy regime. Some disputes may shift back to state court.
  3. Review estate plans for US clients with Brazilian assets. Update wills to take advantage of the limited professio iuris once available.
  4. Map ongoing or threatened homologation proceedings. Substantive review under the new rules may change the calculation.
  5. Train in-house teams. The conceptual shift from rigid connection rules to flexible choice-of-law analysis takes time to internalize.
  6. Build local-counsel relationships now. The reform increases the value of Brazilian counsel who can think comparatively and apply US-style conflict-of-laws reasoning.

Conclusion

Brazil private international law is finally entering the modern era. The 1942 LINDB served its time. The General Law of Private International Law, drafted by some of Brazil’s most respected jurists and academics, replaces it with a framework that respects party autonomy, protects vulnerable parties, and aligns Brazilian doctrine with international consensus. For US counsel, the reform creates both opportunity and risk. Choice-of-law clauses become enforceable in Brazilian state courts. Foreign judgment recognition becomes more substantive.
